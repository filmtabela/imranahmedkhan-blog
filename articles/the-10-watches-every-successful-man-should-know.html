The 10 Watches Every Successful Man Should Know

There is a reason Warren Buffett wears a simple Rolex Datejust worth a fraction of what he could afford, and a separate reason why Bernard Arnault — the man who owns LVMH — wears a Patek Philippe. These are not accidents. These are decisions. And decisions, at the highest levels of wealth and influence, are never accidental.

A watch is the only piece of jewelry a serious man wears without apology. In boardrooms from Mumbai to Manhattan, at private dinners in Geneva, and across the quiet corridors of serious money, a watch communicates what a business card never could. It signals taste, discipline, and the quality of your judgment — before you speak a single word.

The global luxury watch market crossed $9.3 billion in exports from Switzerland alone in 2023, with demand surging from India, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. India's luxury consumption is expected to reach $200 billion by 2030, and watches are at the centre of that shift. A new generation of Indian entrepreneurs, self-made founders, and wealth-conscious professionals are no longer buying watches as status symbols. They are buying them as assets, as statements of identity, and increasingly, as stores of value that outperform traditional portfolios.

This is your essential education. Not a listicle. A curriculum.

---

Why Watches Matter More Than Ever in the New Wealth Economy

Before we examine the watches themselves, understand the architecture of why horology has become central to the global wealth conversation.

In an era of digital everything — crypto wallets, fractional ownership, algorithmic portfolios — the mechanical watch endures as the ultimate counter-cultural asset. It requires no electricity. It cannot be hacked. It has no subscription fee. And in many cases, it appreciates with dignity.

The Rolex Submariner, purchased at retail in 2015 for approximately $7,500, traded on secondary markets in 2022 for over $18,000. Certain Patek Philippe references have returned more per annum than prime real estate in tier-one cities. The Audemars Piguet Royal Oak, launched in 1972 at a provocative price point that shocked the industry, is today one of the most sought-after secondary market watches on earth.

But appreciation is not the only story. The more important point is this: high-net-worth individuals globally treat watches as a parallel currency — liquid, portable, universally recognised. A Rolex Daytona in Tokyo means the same thing as a Rolex Daytona in Dubai or Delhi. That kind of cross-border value recognition is extraordinarily rare.

For the Indian entrepreneur building a global presence, understanding watches is not optional. It is table stakes.

The Foundational Tier: Watches That Define Serious Taste

1. Rolex Submariner — The Universal Language

If you learn one watch, learn this one. The Submariner, introduced in 1953, is the reference point against which all sports luxury watches are measured. It is on the wrist of heads of state, Formula 1 drivers, hedge fund managers, and first-generation founders in Bangalore who have just had their first major exit.

The current generation Submariner (Reference 124110LN) features a 41mm Oystersteel case, Cerachrom bezel, and Rolex's in-house Calibre 3230 movement. It retails at approximately $9,100 but trades significantly above retail. Its waiting lists at authorised dealers are measured in years.

This is not a watch you buy because you can afford it. It is a watch you understand because you have graduated to a certain level of seriousness.

2. Patek Philippe Calatrava — The Connoisseur's Statement

If the Submariner is the handshake, the Calatrava is the conversation. Patek Philippe — founded in Geneva in 1839 — operates at a register above virtually every other watchmaker on earth. Their marketing line, used for decades, is not hyperbole: "You never actually own a Patek Philippe. You merely look after it for the next generation."

The Calatrava is Patek's purist dress watch. Clean dial, time-only or with subtle complications, housed typically in yellow or white gold. It communicates that you have no need to impress anyone. That is, paradoxically, the most impressive thing you can communicate.

3. Audemars Piguet Royal Oak — The Disruptor

In 1972, Gerald Genta designed a sports watch in stainless steel — a "base" material — and priced it higher than gold watches. The industry was appalled. The watch sold out. The Royal Oak permanently altered luxury watchmaking's relationship with material hierarchy.

Today, the Royal Oak Selfwinding (Reference 15500ST) is the watch of technology entrepreneurs, creative founders, and anyone who understands that genuine disruption requires a certain audacity. It is Audemars Piguet's best-known reference, available in steel, gold, and ceramic variants. Secondary market premiums on certain steel configurations exceed 100% of retail.

The Investment Tier: Watches That Build Portfolios

4. Patek Philippe Nautilus — The Ultimate Waitlist Watch

The Nautilus Reference 5711 became so aggressively sought-after that Patek Philippe discontinued the standard steel version in 2021 to manage demand — and the market responded by pushing secondary prices above $150,000 for a watch that retailed at approximately $35,000. This is not speculation. This is documented secondary market data.

The lesson here is not to chase the 5711. The lesson is to understand that genuine scarcity — engineered or organic — creates extraordinary value asymmetry. The Nautilus family continues with new references, and the collecting community remains among the most sophisticated and well-capitalised in the watch world.

5. A. Lange & Söhne Saxonia — The Insider's Watch

If Patek Philippe is the pinnacle of Swiss watchmaking, A. Lange & Söhne occupies an equivalent position in German horology. Founded in Glashütte, Saxony, in 1845 and re-established after the fall of the Berlin Wall in 1990, Lange produces watches of extraordinary technical refinement in quantities that make Patek look prolific.

The Saxonia Thin is the reference that insiders use to identify other insiders. It is not a watch that announces itself. It is a watch that rewards attention. Retail pricing begins around $15,000, secondary market prices hold with extraordinary stability, and ownership places you in a very specific category of collector.

6. Richard Mille RM 11-03 — The Ultra-Modern Flex

Richard Mille is a polarising brand. Critics call it engineered marketing. Advocates — who include Rafael Nadal, Bubba Watson, and a significant proportion of the world's wealthiest individuals — call it the future of horology.

The truth is more nuanced: Richard Mille watches are genuinely extraordinary in engineering terms. Materials like Carbon TPT, NTPT, and titanium alloys borrowed from Formula 1 and aerospace create cases of remarkable rigidity and lightness. The RM 11-03, a flyback chronograph with annual calendar, retails above $150,000 and trades higher. It is the watch that announces, without subtlety, that you have arrived.

Whether that announcement aligns with your personal communication strategy is a decision only you can make. But you must understand this watch exists and what it means.

The Heritage Tier: Watches With Stories That Outlast Trends

7. Omega Speedmaster Professional — History on the Wrist

In 1969, Buzz Aldrin wore an Omega Speedmaster on the lunar surface. NASA's selection process for the moonwatch was rigorous — every major watchmaker of the era submitted watches, and only the Speedmaster survived. This is not marketing mythology. This is documented engineering history.

The Speedmaster Professional "Moonwatch" remains in production today in essentially the same configuration as the one worn on Apollo 11. It retails for approximately $6,500, making it one of the most accessible watches on this list and one of the most historically significant objects available to civilian ownership.

8. IWC Portugieser — The Quiet Aristocrat

IWC Schaffhausen has operated from its factory on the Rhine in Switzerland since 1868. The Portugieser line — originally developed in 1939 for Portuguese navigators who required exceptional accuracy — represents IWC at its most architecturally confident.

The Portugieser Chronograph (Reference IW371601) is particularly significant: a large-diameter watch with a clean, legible dial, column-wheel chronograph movement, and an aesthetic vocabulary that communicates European intellectual seriousness. This is the watch of architects, surgeons, and finance professionals who find the Royal Oak too conspicuous and the Calatrava too understated.

9. Jaeger-LeCoultre Reverso — The Polo Watch

Designed in 1931 for polo players in British India who needed to protect their watch crystals during matches, the Reverso features a case that physically flips to reveal a protective metal caseback. This is horological problem-solving at its most elegant.

The Reverso Grande Seconde is one of the finest executions of the Art Deco aesthetic in watchmaking. It is the watch that connects directly to the Indian subcontinent's relationship with European luxury — a relationship that is now five generations deep. Wearing a Reverso in India carries a particular resonance that no other watch can replicate.

10. Grand Seiko Snowflake — The Eastern Master

SBGA211 — the Grand Seiko Snowflake — is the watch that forces the Western watch world to confront its assumptions. Japanese horology, represented at its peak by Grand Seiko, produces movements of accuracy that rival Swiss chronometers, dials of handcraft that invoke traditional Japanese lacquer and textile arts, and finishing standards that, under any objective examination, equal or exceed what Geneva produces.

The Snowflake features Grand Seiko's Spring Drive movement — a hybrid mechanical-electronic system that achieves accuracy within one second per day — in a case with a dial that replicates the texture of snow-covered terrain in Nagano Prefecture. It retails for approximately $6,500 and is profoundly undervalued relative to its intrinsic quality.

This is the watch for the globally educated collector who has moved beyond European brand prestige into genuine connoisseurship.

Building Your Watch Strategy: How Serious Collectors Think

Understanding individual watches is the foundation. Understanding how to build a collection is the architecture.

The most sophisticated collectors think in terms of function, occasion, and communication. A complete wardrobe typically encompasses: one sports watch (the Submariner or Royal Oak), one dress watch (Calatrava or Saxonia), one historical reference (Speedmaster or Reverso), and one personal obsession — the watch that reflects your individual research and perspective.

Secondary market fluency is non-negotiable. Platforms like Chrono24, WatchBox, and Bob's Watches provide real-time pricing data that serious collectors monitor as attentively as equity prices. The secondary market is where real watch education happens — where you discover which references hold value, which overshoot retail, and which correct back to earth.

For the Indian market specifically: customs duties on imported watches can exceed 35-40% when purchasing internationally, creating complex acquisition decisions. Grey market channels operate at risk, while authorised dealers provide warranty protection and documentation critical to secondary market resale value. Always obtain documentation. Always verify authentication. The certificate and original box contribute meaningfully to eventual resale price.

Authentication matters more than it did five years ago. The proliferation of high-quality counterfeits — particularly from factories in Guangdong province — means that secondary market purchases require either expert examination or a reputable dealer relationship. Build those relationships before you need them.

Key Takeaways

A watch communicates judgment before you speak. Invest in understanding the vocabulary before purchasing any reference.

The 10 watches in this guide — Rolex Submariner, Patek Philippe Calatrava, Audemars Piguet Royal Oak, Patek Philippe Nautilus, A. Lange & Söhne Saxonia, Richard Mille RM 11-03, Omega Speedmaster, IWC Portugieser, Jaeger-LeCoultre Reverso, and Grand Seiko Snowflake — represent the full spectrum of serious horological knowledge.

Not all watches appreciate. But all watches with exceptional provenance, genuine scarcity, and in-house movements demonstrate value stability that most consumer goods cannot match.

The secondary market is your education. Study Chrono24 pricing for 90 days before you buy anything significant. Pattern recognition in this market is a genuine and transferable skill.

For Indian collectors: documentation, authentication, and authorised dealer relationships are your foundation. The grey market carries hidden costs that often exceed apparent savings.

Grand Seiko represents the single most undervalued category in serious watchmaking today. Acquiring knowledge here before the broader market catches up is a genuine and available advantage.

FAQ

Q: What is the best first luxury watch for a successful professional in India?

The Rolex Submariner remains the consensus answer — globally recognised, available in one primary configuration that eliminates paralysis of choice, strong secondary market liquidity, and appropriate for business, travel, and formal occasions. If the waiting list frustrates you, the Omega Seamaster Professional (not on this core list but worth knowing) represents a credible and historically significant alternative at a more accessible price point.

Q: Are luxury watches genuinely good investments?

Select references in select conditions, yes — but this framing misses the point. The Patek Nautilus 5711 and Royal Oak in steel have demonstrated extraordinary returns. But approaching watchmaking as pure investment often leads to acquisitions that satisfy spreadsheets while failing to satisfy the actual purpose of watch ownership, which is daily use and personal resonance. Buy watches you would be proud to wear even if they depreciated. That quality filter, applied consistently, tends to produce the best long-term outcomes.

Q: How does India's customs duty structure affect luxury watch purchasing?

India applies a basic customs duty of 20% on watches, with additional levies including IGST that can bring total duties to 38-42% on the CIF value. This significantly affects the economics of international personal imports. Authorised Indian dealers — Ethos Watch Boutiques, Johnson Watch Company, and Time Avenue among others — provide legitimate acquisition channels with warranty support. For watches purchased abroad and brought into India, honest declaration is the only viable long-term strategy.

Q: What is the difference between Swiss Made and in-house movements?

Swiss Made certification requires that a minimum percentage of manufacturing value occur in Switzerland — a standard that has strengthened in recent years but still permits significant component sourcing from outside Switzerland. In-house movements, by contrast, are designed, manufactured, and assembled within a brand's own facilities. Patek Philippe, A. Lange & Söhne, Rolex, and Audemars Piguet produce genuine in-house calibres. In-house manufacturing is correlated with — though not a guarantee of — superior long-term value retention.

Q: How do I authenticate a pre-owned luxury watch before purchasing?

Four non-negotiable steps: First, verify the reference number and serial number against manufacturer databases or independent resources like Watchbase. Second, examine the movement — if the seller refuses to open the caseback, treat this as a serious red flag. Third, request original purchase documentation and warranty cards — the physical card, not photocopies. Fourth, for any purchase above ₹3 lakhs, commission an independent examination by a qualified watchmaker before completing the transaction. The cost of this examination, typically ₹2,000-5,000, is the most valuable insurance available in the secondary market.
